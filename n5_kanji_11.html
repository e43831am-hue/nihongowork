🖼️ Where did these kanji come from?

Each kanji began as a picture. Follow the journey from real-world image → ancient script → modern kanji for each character in this set.

出
exit / go out / leave
ONシュツ・スイ
KUNで・る・だ・す
Pictograph

The kanji 出 traces its origin to an ancient drawing of a sprouting plant pushing upward from the earth. Early oracle bone script depicted two mountain-like peaks rising from a base, representing a shoot breaking through the soil and emerging into the open air. This powerful image of something pushing outward — breaking free from containment — became the foundation of the character's meaning. As the script evolved through bronze inscriptions and seal script, the double-peak form was stylized into the stacked shapes we recognize today. The upward thrust of the sprout perfectly captures the essence of exiting, going out, or coming forth — making 出 one of the most visually logical kanji in the entire writing system.

入
enter / go in / insert
ONニュウ
KUNい・る・い・れる・は・いる
Pictograph

The character 入 originated from a pictograph depicting a pointed object being driven into the ground — specifically a stake or wedge being inserted downward. In its earliest oracle bone form, the character showed a simple downward-pointing wedge shape, visually representing penetration into a surface. The two converging strokes mirror the physical act of something narrowing as it enters a space — like a key fitting into a lock, or a root pressing into soil. This elegant two-stroke design became one of the most recognized characters in Chinese and Japanese writing. Today, 入 conveys the universal concept of entering, going into, or being inserted into something, retaining the original piercing, inward-moving imagery of its ancient ancestor.

休
rest / break / holiday
ONキュウ
KUNやす・む・やす・まる
Compound Pictograph

The kanji 休 is a beautifully composed compound pictograph combining two distinct images: a person (人) leaning against a tree (木). The left side 亻 is the person radical — a simplified human figure — and the right side 木 is the tree radical, complete with roots and branches. Together they paint a vivid scene: a tired person seeking shade and rest under a tree. This image from ancient China conveyed an immediate sense of pausing, recuperating, and taking a break from labor. The combination is so natural that it has remained largely unchanged for thousands of years. Today 休 is used for resting, vacations, holidays, and any kind of pause — all rooted in that timeless picture of peaceful repose beneath the branches of a tree.

会
meet / assembly / society
ONカイ・エ
KUNあ・う
Compound Ideograph

The kanji 会 (traditional form: 會) originated as a compound ideograph depicting a covered vessel or lid placed over a cooking pot — two things coming together to form a complete, unified whole. The ancient character showed a lid (合) fitting perfectly atop a container, symbolizing the concept of fitting together, joining, or assembling. Over time, this idea of two things uniting was extended to human interaction: when people come together, they meet and form a gathering. The modern simplified form 会 retains the sense of coming together, meeting, and forming a group or society. This kanji appears in essential words for meetings, companies, societies, and opportunities — all situations where separate things or people converge into a unified whole.
